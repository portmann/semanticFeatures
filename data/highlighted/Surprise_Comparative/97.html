From The Ritz To The Ruble: Relaxing With Russia's Currency Depreciation | Seeking Alpha Mar. 12, 2015 9:10 AM ET �|� Includes: ERUS , RBL , RSX , RUSL , RUSS by: The Intercollegiate Finance Journal Today's modern financial markets are complicated and volatile.Currency shocks and negative interest rates, mixed with crisis in the eurozone and crude oil swings, seem to mark a new status quo to which global foreign exchange investors must now adapt.In 2014, these investors around the world watched as the Russian ruble fell nearly 70 percent over the course of the year in response to the ongoing Ukrainian political crisis and slumping crude oil prices.The dollar/ruble pair closed on January 1, 2015 at 68.735 - a significant value considering that the ruble had roughly traded between 25 and 35 since the late 1990s until 2014.This February, however, the ruble has rallied 13 percent against the dollar, and the currency has been the best-performing of the emerging markets' so far this year.Can global markets expect this trend to continue, looking ahead to 2015?In pondering the fate of the ruble in 2015, we must first look at how Vladimir Putin and Russian institutions responded to its fall in 2014.Just as Russia's exporters were hurt by rising prices in 2014, so too were they helped by the ruble fall.No doubt, Putin and his pals in the energy sector were content to let the ruble find its bottom in time.However, though The New York Times reported in December that 81 percent of Russians still supported him, the public is nonetheless wary of rising prices.In the same month, shoppers hurried to stores to buy imported goods whose prices they feared would soon rise, and fast.Regardless of what the poll numbers might imply, it will become difficult for Putin to sell a strong Russia to the public at large if her citizens can't afford to buy many of the things to which they have become accustomed.Putin's popularity and ruble policy have also been marked by an altogether mixed policy from the Central Bank of Russia (CBR).In November, the bank announced that they had "Abolished the exchange rate policy mechanism through cancelling the permissible range of the dual-currency basket..." Essentially, the bank gave up trying to defend the ruble by keeping it within a traded "band" of two prices, and resolved to let the currency find its floor, notwithstanding financial crisis or bank runs.In December, however, the bank hiked the key lending rate by 6.5 percentage points (that's right, whole points) to 17 percent in an effort to stave off inflation and strengthen the ruble; in January, they lowered the rate back to 15 percent "due to the shift in the balance of risks of accelerated consumer price growth and cooling economy."It seems like the CBR is adjusting policy on the fly, and the February ruble rally in the face of such mixed messages is all the more impressive.The conditions that led to the fall of the ruble in 2014 will continue to determine its potential rise in 2015.Last year, Ukraine erupted in strife as large-scale protests led to armed opposition in the nation's eastern parts.Crisis in the Ukraine has continued, if wavering at times, into this year.Donetsk remains a rebel stronghold, its heavily contested airport shelled and shattered.A February 15th ceasefire has been altogether ineffective.However, the rise in the ruble this year in the face of continuing violence in Ukraine and previously enacted sanctions is a positive, contrarian sign for the currency.It doesn't seem like lasting peace will come to the region anytime soon, negotiations have stalled - and yet, Western governments seem reluctant to enact new sanctions.The situation in Ukraine is entrenched, representing a "new normal" to which the ruble is now acclimated.The price of crude oil, on the other hand, can still sway the Russian currency.As Bloomberg notes, the rise in value of the ruble this year has almost mirrored an identical rise in crude.April Brent crude futures closed above $60 a barrel on February 25, at $61.25, a 15 percent gain since February 1.Russia's crude-export intensive economy, long tied to the fate of oil prices, stands to gain from a rise in such prices - and we should expect the ruble to continue to as well.The ruble (black) and April Brent Crude futures (blue), since February 1, 2015 To say that the currency's fate depends solely on the crisis in Ukraine or on the price of crude oil is a simplification, at best, though such a claim does delve into some of the myriad factors influencing the value of the ruble and other exchange rates around the world.What's more, a strong ruble doesn't necessarily imply a strong Russia, and it seems that Putin (together with many of his counterparts around the world) doesn't buy into the fallacy that a strengthened currency belies a strengthened country.We only use your contact details to reply to your request for more information.We do not sell the personal contact data you submit to anyone else.Thank you for your interest in Seeking Alpha PRO We look forward to contacting you shortly for a conversation.Our PRO subscription service was created for fund managers, and the cost of the product is prohibitive for most individual investors.PRO Alerts is our flagship product for individual investors who want to be faster and smarter about their stocks.To learn more about it, click here.If you are an investment professional with over $1M AUM and received this message in error, click here and you will be contacted shortly.We look forward to contacting you when we have an individual investor product ready!
