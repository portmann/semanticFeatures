now you pay 1 SF per dollar.Oops: this represents a 16% drop in the value of the SF against the dollar!So we were wrong, illustrating yet again the perils of forecasting.NOBODY can be consistently right!Wrong for the right reasons.We figured that with all of these geopolitical messes (Ukraine, Middle East), that the SF would represent a safe haven.We also thought that people long of Euros would pour in to the SF; instead, they raced into the dollar.Finally, anecdotal evidence suggests that Swiss exporters got so worried about the SF that business ground to a temporary halt, hurting the economy's prospects.Who wants to be long of a currency moving into a rotten Economic Time�, an "excess supply of goods"?Cash tax.As you know, holdings of SF cash (i.e. excluding SF stocks and bonds) command negative interest of 0.75% per annum.At present, the Swiss bank has to pay the Central Bank, Swiss National Bank (SNB), 0.75% on its cash holdings, i.e. on customers' deposits.My Swiss friend tells me that as of 1st April, individual holders of SF cash will have to pay these 75 basis points; this means that the payee is switched from the bank to the individual.Operationally, they pay the bank whom they have deposited their SF cash with; the bank, in turn, passes this 75 basis point tax take on to the SNB.SNB to raise cash tax more?My Swiss friend further confided that if the SF were to rise despite that 75 basis point tax on cash, then the SNB will raise this cash tax even more... obviously the SNB does not want the SF to strengthen!Investment implications.I cannot imagine people paying in order to hold SFs.Thus, expect them to pile out of SF - probably in to dollars.That puts a further damper on the SF.Thus, short the SF.But if you simply must have SF exposure, here is a way to mitigate your forex losses: given the questionable market belief that a weak currency is good for exporters , buy bombed-out Swiss exporters in the expectation of a weaker SF.Alternately, buy SF-quoted stocks that have huge dollar exposure;
