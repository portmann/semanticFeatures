Job Openings Rise to the Highest Level in 14 Years - Real Time Economics - WSJ Job seekers wait in line to register for a job fair at California�s Great America theme park on Feb. 6, 2015, in Santa Clara, Calif.Employers across the U.S. had 5 million job openings at the end of January, the most since January 2001, providing further hope that the labor market is on the mend.Openings have been rising across a range of industries, according to the Labor Department�s Job Openings and Labor Turnover Survey, known as Jolts.Professional and business services, health care and accommodation and food services have seen openings rise; mining and logging, which includes the beleaguered U.S. oil industry, has seen openings decline.The level of hiring declined slightly in January, falling to 5 million from 5.2 million in December.The total number of people leaving their job declined slightly, too�separations fell to 4.8 million from 4.9 million.The Labor Department’s main jobs report shows the net change in jobs— a gain of 295,000  in February—but the Jolts report digs one level deeper, cataloging the millions of workers each month who quit a job or are laid off, retire, start a new job or switch jobs.The Jolts report is closely followed by the Federal Reserve.Fed Chairwoman  Janet Yellen  has identified the report’s tally of the number of people who voluntarily quit their jobs each month as an important barometer of the economy’s health.In a good labor market, workers are more willing to quit—owing to greater confidence in their prospects or greater opportunities to find different work.The number of workers voluntarily quitting their job climbed to 2.8 million.Despite improvement in recent months, the level of quits has yet to recover to its prerecession levels.Job openings,  hiring and quitting all decline during recessions and then gradually recover.The measures never fully recovered from the 2001 recession and took a huge hit during the recession from 2007-09.While job openings are getting close to pre-2001 levels, the rate of quitting and hiring remains more than 10% below, a clear sign that the dynamic labor market of the late 1990s and early 2000s remains out of reach for now.The number of unemployed workers per job opening is close to where it was before the most recent recession.For every job opening, there are 1.8 unemployed people.By the end of the recession, there were close to seven unemployed workers for every job opening.As close followers of labor market data know, the unemployment rate only includes those who are actively searching for work.It does not include so-called marginally attached workers, who may be so discouraged by the economy that they have given up looking for a job.It also does not include people with part-time jobs who would like to work full time.When you tally up this broader measure of underemployment, there are 3.8 workers for every available job opening—not quite as close to prerecession levels.Labor market economists love to track the Beveridge Curve, which shows the relationship between the job openings rate and the unemployment rate.Before the recession that began in December 2007, this relationship was steady.If job openings climbed, the unemployment rate tended to fall.During the recession, the unemployment rate shot to 10% and the availability of job openings plunged.Then, in the recovery, the openings rate began to climb, but unemployment remained high.The new job listings were not being filled by the vast number of unemployed workers.This relationship has still not returned to where it was in the mid-2000s.The last time there were as many job openings as today, the unemployment rate was under 4.5%.This suggests that, for whatever reason, workers are not filling the available jobs as easily as in the past.This could be a sign that workers lack the skills for available openings or that employers are unwilling to pay sufficient salaries to get workers to accept
