Includes: DRR , ERO , EUFX , EUO , FORX , FXE , UDN , UDNT , ULE , URR , USDU , UUP , UUPT by: Bob McTeer In recent years, in response to those who complained about our "weak" dollar and longed for "King" dollar, I have frequently paraphrased Saint Augustine on the topic of chastity.He reportedly prayed for the Lord to make him chaste, but not just yet.Likewise, I said I wanted a stronger dollar, but not just yet.When a healthy economy has a strong currency, its citizens benefit from a higher standard of living through lower import prices and a positive terms of trade (more imports per unit of export).If the economy is struggling to stimulate its way out of a recession or sluggish growth, that benefit will likely be outweighed by the headwinds a strong currency brings on exports.One question to ask regarding the recent dollar appreciation is whether the U.S. economy is finally strong enough to bear it.My guess is that it is strong enough to accommodate a moderate appreciation of the dollar, but not a 17 percent appreciation.Like the decline in oil prices, dollar appreciation has been too far, too fast.Of course, to a large extent the strong dollar is just a reflection of a weak euro, which is needed to keep the Eurozone out of a triple dip recession and avoid deflation.However, there is no offsetting currency appreciation against the dollar anywhere in the world.Our two largest trading partners, Canada and Mexico, also have currencies depreciating against the dollar.We are in the position of having to acquiesce to the whole world's efforts to stimulate exports at our expense.Extrapolating recent trends means the euro could fall to parity with the dollar soon.There is nothing wrong with that except that the euro was recently trading in the high $1.30s, and that is quite a sharp change in a short time.The markets may well be overshooting the rate that would restore equilibrium over time.As for the level, the euro started out at a discount to the dollar, and there is no good reason that its subsequent premium to the dollar was what nature intended.After the dollar is the dollar.I don't have a remedy to offer.If the monetary authorities of the Euro-zone and other depreciating countries were driving their exchange rates down through intervention in the foreign exchange markets, it would be considered overt beggar-my-neighbor behavior.No doubt, the monetary easing taking place by the Bank of Japan and the ECB is needed for domestic economic goals and their depreciating currencies are a by-product - probably a welcome by-product - but a by
