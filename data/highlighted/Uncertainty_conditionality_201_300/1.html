A Look At Greece's Impact On The Euro | Seeking Alpha Economic growth in Greece is steadily rising.Meanwhile, the labor market has also stabilized the last few years.With deflation still a concern in Greece, as well as in the overall currency bloc, however, the euro should remain under selling pressure.The Greek economy is improving, but still has room to recover, signaling more support will be needed from external sources in future quarters.The euro is represented by CurrencyShares Euro Trust (NYSEARCA: FXE ).Economic growth in Greece has drastically improved in recent quarters.In the fourth quarter, the economic growth figure came in at an annual pace of 1.2%, below the previous quarter's reading of 1.6%, as well as missing estimates for 1.7%.Since 2011, however, economic growth has rebounded from -10% annual contraction, to current levels, seen below.Economic conditions could further improve in coming months as both ECB stimulus and low consumer prices stimulate activity ."The economy is likely to gain momentum in the coming quarters.The drop in oil prices, the lower euro and easing bank lending conditions bode well for growth, while the ECB's quantitative easing program should improve financial conditions further," Nick Kounis of ABN Amro told the Independent.Additionally, Greece's labor market has also strengthened.In the third quarter, the employment figure came in at an annual pace of -0.56% contraction, well above the previous quarter's reading of -1.88%.Since bottoming in 2012 at close to -10% annual contraction, employment has increased steadily.Although employment is improving at a faster pace, the total level of jobs in the economy is well below pre-financial crisis levels.This could provide headwinds to economic growth in the future.Lastly, inflation in the country, much like the rest of the euro area, continues to fall.In February, the inflation figure came in at an annual pace of -2.2% contraction, above the previous month's reading of -2.8%, as well as exceeding estimates for -2.5%.While inflation measures are stabilizing, the negative downward pressure persists.Since peaking at nearly 6% in 2010, the inflation measure has drastically fallen, seen below.With negative inflation still in effect, policymakers will be forced to keep a bias towards easing.Looking at Greece's economy, it is apparent that conditions are improving, but within a relative context.There is still a lot of room for improvement, but economic activity and the labor market have emerged from the depths of the 2011 recession.With policymakers committed to policy easing until inflation rises and economic activity further strengthens around the currency bloc, the euro should remain under selling pressure against many of its
