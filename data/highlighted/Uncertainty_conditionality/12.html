Mutual Funds - Is Past Performance A Guide To The Future?| Seeking Alpha Mar. 12, 2015 11:31 AM ET ?|? Includes: DFCVX , DFLVX , FAGIX , FDVLX , FHYTX , FSLBX , FSRBX , HOVLX , NICSX , NSEIX , OTCAX , OTCFX , PFOAX , PRNHX , RPFGX , RPMGX , TIFUX , TOPPX , VIVAX , VWEHX by: Philip Baker A portfolio of the five best performing funds selected on the basis of past performance outperformed a simple broad-based portfolio of 20 funds and the S&P 500.Using five or less years of past data to determine the top five best performing funds yielded better results than using 10 years of data.The better the past performance, the better has been the forward performance of mutual fund portfolios.Over the last 10 years, the S&P 500 has delivered a compound annual return (including dividends) of 9.5% with volatility of 18.4%, giving a return/risk ratio of 50.8%.Over the same period, an equally weighted portfolio of the 20 mutual funds shown in the table below returned 9.8% with volatility of 18.8% to give a return/risk ratio of 52.2%.Our quest was to test whether a (concentrated) portfolio of funds selected on the basis of superior past risk adjusted performance would outperform a simple broad based portfolio of mutual funds.20 years of data was used.We determined the compound annual return (including distributions), annualized volatility and return/volatility ratio for each fund for 10 years up to each day of the last 10 years (2005-2015).We ranked the funds each day of the 10 years to 3/10/2014 by return/volatility for the preceding 10 years.We then determined the return for each subsequent 12 month period for the portfolio of 5 top ranked funds, plus the other three quartiles.For the last 10 years (to 3/10/2015), we therefore had a series of forward year's returns for portfolios of each performance quartile of funds.From this, we were able to determine the return/risk ratio over the 10 years for each quartile.To test whether the period over which the past performance was measured for ranking purposes would have any bearing on the results, we tested five-year and one-year evaluation periods as well as 10 year.There is a positive correlation between fund performance history and returns for the subsequent 12 months, for the one, five and 10 year periods used to determine the performance on which the rankings were based Despite being more concentrated, the top quartile generated a higher return/risk ratio than applied to the set of 20 funds (and to the S&P 500) Better results for the top quartile were obtained where five years or one year of history were used to rank the funds, than when 10 years were used.We therefore find some merit in a strategy of selecting a portfolio of mutual funds on the basis of past risk adjusted performance.As of the time of writing, the current top five funds for 10, five and one year analysis periods are shown below: Source:
