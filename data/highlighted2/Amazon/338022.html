In a previous article on Netflix , I instigated that Netflix UK had an inferior content library and was threatened by Amazon . Some commenters argued that Netflix UK would soon enough catch up with the US library.At around 11 % content growth YoY , that will only happen if Netflix US shrinks even more . (Surprise & Uncertainty) The Netflix catalog is not a monotonically increasing function.For instance , in 2016 , there were 352 additions versus 324 removals so far , for a total net inflow of just 28 titles , which suggests a significant slowdown in growth . (Uncertainty) This, of course, reflects the company's increasing capital expenditures for growing its content, the costs of which have been pointed out by other contributors.These numbers of course do not allow qualitative statements about the new content compared to the removed content.As we know , Netflix is pushing massively toward producing more of its own shows , e.g. recently Fuller House . (Surprise) However, I would invite readers to look at the latest additions and removals for themselves, which brings me to my next point.The vast majority of Netflix titles stay on for no more than 12 months , according to the tracking site . (Surprise) The release year with the highest number of titles available is 2014.In my opinion , we can roughly summarize the content strategy , aside from original content , as follows : Permanent additions to the library are often cheaper/dated or niche content . (Uncertainty) The core of the library (again, aside from originals), are 2-to-5-year old movies that are licensed for a limited amount of time, hence a slow-down in net-influx.Now , Netflix would prefer if you did not notice this in too much detail . (Uncertainty) In my opinion, this explains the weirdly-designed search function.It also explains the lack of a list view, which would be the easiest thing in the world to implement.However , it would also give users an easy overview of the library and make them realize how weak the content just might be . (Uncertainty) Instead, Netflix displays a rotating view of whatever it wants you to see.Again, I know the counterargument to this is supposed to be Netflix's original content, which is heavily advertised.There is only one problem with that.Not everyone cares for it.In the UK, according to a survey carried out last year, two thirds reported they were not watching original programming, but rather US TV shows and movies.Of course , this might also change in the future as more original content is created . (Surprise & Uncertainty) However , I think there is still a big risk associated with Netflix strategy , which I will now summarize . (Uncertainty) Whenever I talk to Netflix users here in the UK, most of them are not especially happy with the content.My analysis from above explains why.Users might intuitively feel `` there is nothing I want to watch . '' (Uncertainty) They might not understand this is the result of Netflix 's presumed deliberate attempts to hide its sparse library behind its layout without an option to list it in full . (Uncertainty) What we are seeing is a "Fake it till you make it" strategy.Netflix cannot afford to bring up its libraries in all regions to US level.As we saw, the overall content influx is slowing down as a result to its expensive shift to an original content producer.This does not mean Netflix will not continue to sign up subscribers.What it means is the reason Netflix is able to do so is because right now, the main thing Netflix has going for it in most countries is that ... it is there.It should also be noted that the UK was one of the earlier markets Netflix expanded into , so the situation in other European countries is presumably worse . (Surprise & Uncertainty) While piling up expenses to produce more content , the company has become vulnerable . (Surprise) As soon as viable competitors become more broadly available in other markets ( Amazon , Hulu and HBO probably being among the first ) , I continue to expect a major correction , potentially triggered by a loss in US subscribers .
