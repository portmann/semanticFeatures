(Amazon: Uncertainty) This past weekend, I spent some time listening to some archived TED talks through my NPR One app and came across a fabulous TED talk about collaboration.One of the speakers was Margaret Heffernan , who is an entrepreneur, executive consultant, and author.Margaret told a fascinating story about a Purdue University evolutionary biologist named William Muir.Mr. Muir created an elaborate scientific study to track the productivity of chicken flocks.I will paraphrase the story, and for interested readers, the full story can be found here .Before you stop reading and say that I 'm `` off the reservation '' crazy , I would argue that lessons from this experiment are warning signs for Amazon ( NASDAQ : AMZN ) investors . Essentially, Mr. Muir devised an empirical study where he tracked two flocks of chicken over the course of six generations (according to Wikipedia - the average chicken life lasts seven to eight years).The first flock was a team of average "worker bee" chickens that were steady egg producers.The second flock was a team of superstar, alpha chickens at the top of their game measured by their egg production.Over the course of six generations, he tracked, monitored, and plotted the progress of the "worker bee" flock and the "superstar" flock.What he found at the end of the experiment was that the flock of average chickens far surpassed the superstar flock of chicken in every category.In fact, by the end of the experiment, only three sixth generation superstar chickens were alive.The others all died from poor health.Perhaps , this was the result of the caustic flock chemistry and individual ambition over the best interest of the team . (Uncertainty) Also, the only three surviving chickens were in poor health, measuring by weight, behavior, and evaluating their feathers.The worker bees ended up thriving in a more hospitable environment .
