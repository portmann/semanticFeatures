Boeing share price looks undervalued on the absolute basis. Boeing (NYSE: BA ) is one of the most famous US companies, producer of aircrafts, rockets and satellites for both civilian and military use. Based on 2013 revenues it was the second largest defense contractor and the biggest US exporter. Founded in 1910 it was one of the pioneers in the industry, the company's centennial operation has made it one of the most successful US companies. Recent higher demand of commercial aircrafts has increased Boeing's profits, 26% in 2013. Gross profit of the company in the first quarter of 2015 was $ 3.653 billion. The growth of passenger traffic and decrease in the price of oil has made the aviation industry very attractive in 2014 and it was a very successful year and the trends will likely continue in 2015. Only in 2014 over 1,490 jet airplanes were delivered, and airlines ordered approximately 3,680 new airplanes. Boeing is forecasting a need for 38,050 airplanes valued at more than $5.6 trillion in the next 20 years. Bearing in mind that Boeing is also operating in military and space industries, the company has a bright future. On June 22, 2015 the board of directors declared a regular quarterly dividend of ninety-one ($0.91) cents per share. To understand Boeing's prospects as a long-term investment, we take a step back and estimate the intrinsic value of the stock based on their ability to generate cash flows. To complement the analysis, we will also asses the relative richness/cheapness of Boeing's ordinary stock compared to their peers. Our valuation model for Boeing is based on the company's ability to generate cash. The key measure of cash flows that we use is Free cash flow, which is the total cash inflow from operations minus the dividends and capital expenditure outlays. This is effectively the amount of "excess" cash that the company is making and therefore accruing to its lenders and shareholders. While we do look at historic cash flows, cash flows projections are of crucial importance because markets are forward looking. We start with the analyst consensus Free Cash Flow projections. For our own projection, we assume that the analyst consensus is correct for the next 5 fiscal years. After that, we assume that the growth rate of cash flows falls back to its previous trend and remains on it for the next 5 years, after which it normalizes towards the sustainable rate of 3.95% per annum, based on the average real GDP growth over the last 15 years and the average inflation rate of 2%. To calculate the total value of the firm, we discount the projected cash flows and the company's terminal value by its weighted average cost of capital ( OTC:WACC ). Our estimate of Boeing's cost of debt is 4.71%, based on their interest expense and amount of debt outstanding in the last fiscal year. The cost of equity is calculated using the 10 year treasury yield as the "risk-free" proxy plus the implied equity risk premium of 8.11% times the historic beta of 1.0026 for the stock.
