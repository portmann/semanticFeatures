Apple: Not All Vertical Integrations Are Created Equal - Apple Inc. (NASDAQ:AAPL) | Seeking Alpha Apple and Samsung have become the dominant players in the premium smartphone tier. This dominance is due in large part to the greater vertical integration of the companies relative to competitors. The differences in approach to vertical integration in the two companies indicates that Samsung will never overcome Apple's advantage in mobile device profitability. Samsung's ( OTC:SSNLF ) June quarter earnings release contained a steep y/y decline in its Internet and Mobile (IM) division operating profit of 37%, while IM revenue declined by 8%. Apple (NASDAQ: AAPL ) and Samsung are considered the dominant players in smartphones due to their vertical integration. But the difference in profitability between the companies demonstrates that not all vertical integrations are created equal. Based on Samsung's decline in smartphone unit shipments that I wrote about in Samsung's Glancing Blow , it was evident that revenue for the IM division was going to be lower. IM includes some Internet related equipment sales, but the bulk of the revenue comes from Samsung's smartphones, tablets, and feature phones. In this article, I'll use IM as a proxy for its mobile device business. What wasn't clear was how much operating profit decline IM would suffer as a result. From the above results, it appears that Samsung's Galaxy S6 and S6 Edge not only delivered an ineffectual blow against Apple, to some degree, the blow ricocheted, damaging Samsung's mobile device profitability. Judging by Qualcomm's (NASDAQ: QCOM ) recent earnings call , both Apple and Samsung are considered dominant in the upper tier of the smartphone market. This is arguably due to the greater level of vertical integration that these companies have achieved relative to their competition. Both companies design and engineer their own smartphones and tablets. Both companies go beyond this to design and engineer their own systems on chip ((SOCs)) that power these devices. Custom SOCs constitute an important discriminator for both Apple and Samsung mobile devices. Beyond custom SOCs, the vertical integrations of the two companies diverge in important ways. Samsung has long maintained its own semiconductor fabrication capability, and builds its own SOCs. Apple is dependent on outside foundries, among them Samsung itself. Apple deploys its own mobile device operating system, based on Mac OS X. Samsung has developed its own mobile device operating system, Tizen, but mostly relies on Google's (NASDAQ: GOOG ) (NASDAQ: GOOGL ) Android for its smartphones and tablets. Does the competitive advantage conferred by Samsung's foundry capability outweigh the competitive advantage of Apple's self-developed iOS? The table above clearly indicates that it does not. The mobile device operating system is the key to the user experience. The mobile OS brings together all the fundamental elements of the modern mobile device ecosystem, including apps, services, and entertainment content. Samsung's dependence on Google for Android is in many ways a carryover of the commodity PC paradigm. The OS developer for commodity computing devices develops a generic OS, and then throws it over the fence to the hardware makers. The resultant user experience is less than optimal. In the PC world, users put up with it because it lowered the cost of owning a PC. Obviously, that's still true in the commodity mobile device market, but the usability penalties for the approach are more severe. Partly this has to do with the greater variety of mobile device hardware platforms. Partly this has to do with the technical challenges of tinkering with a mobile device operating system. Most mobile device users never tinker with either their hardware or OS, but simply live with what they've bought. In assessing Apple's competitive position going forward, the advantage conferred by iOS is often overlooked. Apple critics continue to maintain that Android (or Windows Phone) is "just as good" as iOS. If that were really true, surely Samsung would be in a much better position than it is. Part of the problem is really not the fault of Android as a mobile OS, but is due to the structural relationship between Google and its manufacturing partners. To some extent, Google siphons off profit from the manufacturers in the form of Android advertising and services revenue. Google keeps the profit from Google Play, for instance, and Samsung gets nothing. If we accept that Apple's control of iOS provides it a competitive advantage, then this says something very important about Apple's long-term prospects in mobile devices. This is because the situation with respect to Android is unlikely to change. The current Android device makers are unlikely to successfully develop and deploy their own operating systems or even switch to an alternative. Android device makers have made Windows Phone devices, but these hardly constitute a viable alternative to Android in terms of sales volume. The device makers are pretty much stuck with Android for the foreseeable future. This means that no manufacturer in the Android ecosystem has a path forward to overcome the iOS advantage.
