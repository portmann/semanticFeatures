Let us first discuss the positive factors. First, FSFR trades at a material discount to my projected NAV as of 9/30/2015 (per share figure provided near the end of the article). As of 11/27/2015, the stock price was $8.74 per share. This calculates to a discount of ($3.19) per share for a price-to-NAV ratio of 0.7324 (a 26.76% discount). I believe some investors would see this material discount to NAV as an attractive opportunity to own FSFR. As such, even though it had an annual forward yield to its NAV as of 6/30/2015 of 7.36%., the company had an annual forward yield to its stock price as of 11/27/2015 of 10.30%. I believe this current yield, which is currently in excess of 10.00%, is one positive factor of owning FSFR. Second, even though the company materially reduced its monthly dividend per share rate beginning in September 2015 (which was a negative trend when it occurred), the new, more cautious, dividend per share rate has a higher probability of being maintained over a long period of time. I believe investors will eventually "come to terms" with the new, more cautious, dividend and ultimately have more comfort knowing that another decrease in the future is a fairly low probability. Third, FSFR announced several quarters ago it had formed a joint venture ("JV") partnership with members of the Glick Family (GF Funding) to expand the company's investment portfolio. This newly created JV partnership is called "FSFR Glick Family JV LLC" (FSFR Glick JV), and was formed during the fiscal first quarter of 2015. FSFR has continued to state the company expects to generate an annual return in the low-teens in regard to this recently formed JV partnership. When fully funded, FSFR Glick JV is expected to generate annual returns approximately 500 basis points ("bps") above FSFR's average return on investment ("ROI"). This is due to the fact that management fees are based on the net assets of FSFR Glick JV (not gross assets), which is one strategic advantage. In addition, by utilizing JV partnerships, FSFR can "circumvent" the business development company ("BDC") debt-to-equity leverage ratio threshold of 1.0x, while still maintaining its Regulated Investment Company ("RIC") status per the Internal Revenue Code ("IRC"). By the end of June 2015, FSFR Glick JV had $147.6 million in assets consisting of senior secured loans. This funding has been provided through equity by both partners and through the JV's $200 million revolving credit facility with Credit Suisse AG, Cayman Islands (Credit Suisse). Management anticipates FSFR Glick JV will have an investment capacity of approximately $300 million. As such, the JV was not fully "ramped up" during the fiscal fourth quarter of 2015. As it continues to grow, the benefits of this type of investment structure should begin to have a more material impact towards the end of 2015 and into the calendar year 2016. Management has also stated FSFR will likely have future discussions with other interested third parties to create additional JV partnerships over the next several years. I believe the strategic advantages of these types of JV partnerships should be seen as a positive factor. Fourth, FSFR has recently continued to expand the company's available borrowings. In June 2015, management announced the refinancing of the company's previously amended $200 million Natixis/Fifth Third Bank Revolving Credit Facility/Term Loans by closing a $309 million "debt securitization". In conjunction with this event, FSFR issued $223 million of long-term notes, which are secured by the company's underlying portfolio investments. These long-term notes are termed the "2015 Notes" and have five different "classes". The weighted average interest rate associated with the five classes of 2015 Notes is three-month LIBOR plus 2.01%, which is a slight improvement when compared to the weighted average of the existing $200 million Natixis/Fifth Third Bank Revolving Credit Facility/Term Loans. The debt securitization has a four-year reinvestment period, a two-year non-call period, and a ten-year maturity. As such, FSFR has extended the maturity of a material portion of the company's borrowings at attractive debt financing rates. Even though an increase in LIBOR will cause an increase to FSFR's cost of funds rate, the company has nearly 100% of its investment portfolio in senior secured, floating-rate debt investments. As such, any increase in interest expense will be "trumped" by an increase in interest income once FSFR's weighted average cash floor is surpassed. Furthermore, in all likelihood, net interest spreads will increase as the "base rate" on all new lower middle market ("LMM") and middle-market ("MM") loans will be at a higher stated rate in a rising interest rate environment. Fifth, in relation to the point above, during the fiscal third quarter of 2015, FSFR wrote off the unamortized portion of deferred costs in relation to the company's existing $200 million Natixis/Fifth Third Bank Revolving Credit Facility/Term Loans. This "one-time" expense caused the company's NII to net decrease by ($0.03) per share during the fiscal third quarter of 2015. This was due to the fact FSFR reported a material increase in quarterly interest expense partially offset by lower quarterly incentive fees. I am anticipating no such event occurred during the fiscal fourth quarter of 2015, which would be considered a positive factor. Sixth, management has continued to reiterate FSFR does not anticipate issuing shares of common stock below CURRENT NAV for the foreseeable future. Even though I have stated I was against the August 2014 equity offering (due to the fact that it was materially dilutive to existing shareholders; at the time, I did not own this stock), I am comforted management will not perform any further dilutive actions for the foreseeable future. I believe this notion has been solidified by the recent expansion of the company's available debt financing, which was discussed earlier. As such, I believe FSFR not performing any dilutive equity offerings for the foreseeable future continues to be another positive factor. Finally, the material decrease in the price of oil during FSFR's fiscal first and fourth quarters of 2015 has caused "distress"/speculation to surround most of the oil & gas sector (including parts of the energy sector) regarding falling margins and potential struggling/bankrupt entities. While I would agree the markets should have some sort of generalized "cause for concern" within the high-yield debt market, I also believe one needs to "drill down" to each BDC's debt and equity investments to see which portfolio companies could be affected by this event. As of 12/31/2014, only 0.78% of FSFR's portfolio had debt and equity investments within the oil & gas sector (including certain investments in the energy sector which had "oil & gas" characteristics). As of 6/30/2015, this percentage remained relatively unchanged at 0.71%. When compared to the ten other BDC peers I currently cover, this was one of the lowest percentages at potential risk for investment depreciation due to falling crude oil/natural gas prices. I believe FSFR's continued extremely low exposure to the oil & gas sector (percentage-wise) should be seen as a positive factor. Now, let us discuss three cautionary/negative factors. The first cautionary/negative factor is in relation to FSFR's external manager, Fifth Street Asset Management Inc. (NASDAQ: FSAM ), and the company's affiliate, Fifth Street Finance Corp. (NASDAQ: FSC ). Currently, FSAM, FSC, and certain members of each entity's prior/current executive management team/officers are defendants in several class action lawsuits where plaintiffs have alleged the defendants knowingly/fraudulently inflated asset valuations of several of FSC's portfolio companies prior to FSAM's "initial public offering" ("IPO"). While the lawsuit DOES NOT state there was any "wrongdoing" on the part of FSFR, it should be noted the company's executive management team/officers are stated as defendants in these lawsuits, as certain individuals also manage FSC's investment portfolio. As such, these current lawsuits may indirectly impact FSFR's business operations regarding management's time devoted to the case (for instance, meeting with legal counsel, appearances in court to testify, etc.) and the potential for an eventual change in the executive management team/officers. At this point in time, I believe it is only prudent to consider the impacts this event may have regarding FSFR. Second, it was recently disclosed that FSAM erroneously calculated FSC's and FSFR's portion of incentive fees (account description at the BDC level) over a period of several years. These erroneous calculations occurred because FSC and FSFR revised each company's accounting treatment of certain fees when loans were originated. Over the several years I covered FSC and FSFR, management continued to "front-load" certain advisory, servicing, and/or structuring fees in relation to loan originations. In prior articles, I have noted this was more of an "aggressive stance" regarding revenue recognition under Generally Accepted Accounting Principles ("GAAP"). I also stated the more cautious/consistent treatment of such fees were to recognize revenue over the estimated life of the applicable loan. It is somewhat discouraging that Fifth Street's external auditors over this time period did not "catch" this error sooner, since an unqualified opinion continued to be provided for all affected companies. An unqualified opinion simply means the external auditors did not see any "material misstatements" within FSAM's, FSC's, and/or FSFR's financial statements (and all disclosures) during each company's annual audit. So, in other words, due to each company's change in accounting treatment, FSC and FSFR overstated certain portions of revenue, while overpaying each company's respective share of incentive fees for several years. With that being said, since FSC and FSFR made prior period adjustments to the recognition of certain revenue, partially offset by a reduction in incentive fees paid to FSAM, both company's prior period financial statements were negatively impacted for most quarters (a net "reverse effect" occurred during the fiscal third quarter of 2015). Since this is a revenue recognition "timing" issue, in future quarters both FSC and FSFR should actually record additional revenue from these specific loans, since revenue is being recognized over the estimated life of the loan versus recognizing the entire amount of applicable fees at origination. A projection of FSFR's prior period adjustments and the impact on the company's prior period NAV (which will be recorded during the current quarter) will be provided later in the article. Third, consistent with most BDC peers during the quarter ending 9/30/2015, I anticipate FSFR will record a notable amount of net depreciation within the company's investment portfolio during the fiscal fourth quarter of 2015. This is due to market volatility and the negative pricing of loans in the LMM, MM, and especially, the syndicated loan market. As such, I believe this is a short-term negative factor when the company reports quarterly results.
