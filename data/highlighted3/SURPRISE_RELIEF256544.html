Will ConocoPhillips Reduce This Key Target With Its 2016 Operating Plan?- ConocoPhillips (NYSE:COP) | Seeking Alpha Stone Fox Capital , Stone Fox Capital (1,614 clicks) ConocoPhillips continues to increase production into an extremely weak market for energy products..The cuts in capital spending aren't targeted a reducing production..The odd story with ConocoPhillips (NYSE: COP ) and the E&P firms in general is that the sector rallied over the last few months.At the same time, oil prices ticked below $40 per barrel this week.The ironic part of the story is that the energy market hasn't done anything to solve the over supply situation.In fact, despite the greatly reduced capital spending plans by market participants firms like ConocoPhillips continue to forecast solid production growth.My research warned the planned production growth in the face of lower oil prices was a big problem when the year started.The spending cuts are generally tied to lower drilling costs along with a focus swift to the more prolific, short-cycle shale wells that has kept a fundamental focus on production growth.The question though is why investors want to chase these stocks higher.ConocoPhillips still needs much higher oil prices to cover capital spending and dividends.The reality exists that the target oil price could rise again when drilling costs bounce higher with a market rebound.The fundamental problem with the stock is that ConocoPhillips along with most in the industry are reactionary.The company isn't making moves to position the business for the future.The reaction to lower oil price is to cut capital spending as oil prices fall and to shift drilling projects towards the short-cycle, shale types.No more investing for the future or planning production for coming online in a few years when oil prices will likely rebound.Maybe most telling is the below slide for the recent Merrill Lynch Energy Conference.The company remains flexible with capital reductions when investing in the business, but ConocoPhillips still raised the dividend.Source: BOA Merrill Lynch Energy Conference presentation The likely outcome of the 2016 Operating Plan call on December 10 is a further reduction in capital spending.The lower oil prices will again cause the company to cut spending going forward.With WTI prices averaging nearly $51 this year through Q3, ConocoPhillips has increased debt by $2.4 billion while reducing the cash balance by $2.7 billion.The Q3 results weren't much better even with reduced spending.The company still spent more on capex and dividends than cash generated by operations to the tune of $1.5 billion.The problem continues to exist that the large cut in capex from the 2014 levels did nothing to reduce production.If anything, the shift to short-cycle, shale projects from long-cycle, deepwater projects helped shift production forward exactly when the company didn't need it.As the slide below highlights, ConocoPhillips repeatedly cut capital spending targets for this year, yet the company still beat the initial production guidance.Source: ConocoPhillips Q3'15 presentation When the company releases the 2016 plans next Thursday, the discussion needs to center around production cuts or curtailments.At this point, capital spending and operational cost cuts don't matter.What needs to happen is a reversal of the production growth.ConocoPhillips lists itself as the world's largest independent E&P firm based on production and proved reserves.The company needs to lead the industry by introducing the concept of reducing supply to the market when benchmark prices are below certain thresholds.Oil and natural gas are valuable commodities to sell them indiscriminately at depressed prices.The key takeaway is that lower spending combined with production growth only plays a part in the race to the bottom.Absent any surprise news at the meeting next week , investors should avoid ConocoPhillips and actually use the recent rally to dump the stock .
