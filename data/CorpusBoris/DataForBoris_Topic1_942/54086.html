Grand Central: Time for the Red Queen to Stop? - Real Time Economics - WSJ And so it continues. Seven years on from the financial crisis, and six years on from the Great Recession, central banks around the world continue to cut their benchmark interest rates or otherwise ease monetary policy. Romania’s central bank was the latest of these, lowering its key rate Tuesday, following Australia by a few hours. Norway’s central bank declined to make it a trifecta Wednesday, but hinted that it may cut at its next meeting. Another potential easer is New Zealand. In the first half of last year, its central bank hiked four times, and was seen as a pathfinder for others on the way towards normalizing rates. But as most of the rest of Asia has eased policy-a strengthening New Zealand dollar has contributed to a sharp decline in inflation. Meanwhile, the European Central Bank, the Bank of Japan and the People’s Bank of China continue to provide fresh stimulus. There seems to be a Red Queen effect in some of this, with central banks having to act just to stay in the same place. As inflation rates fall, real interest rates rise, and more stimulus is needed to maintain the status quo. The same principle applies to currency movements–as New Zealand’s predicament shows, leaving policy unchanged while the national currency appreciates sharply is the same as tightening. The Federal Reserve and the Bank of England are rare examples of institutions that seem comfortable with that course in 2015. But the persistence of abnormally stimulative monetary policy raises some fundamental questions. The early months of 2015 suggest the global economy won’t grow much more quickly this year than it did in 2014, and may even slow. Inflation rates seem to have stopped falling, but they are still very low. What central banks have been doing for the past seven years doesn’t seem to have removed the need for more of what central banks have been doing. It seems increasingly possible that central banks should have been doing something different, and that what they’ve been doing is part of the problem.
