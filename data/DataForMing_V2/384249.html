Apple: Weak Quarter, Weaker Outlook Coming - Apple Inc. (NASDAQ:AAPL) | Seeking Alpha TrendForce estimates iPhone March-quarter market share plunging to 14.4% from 20.9% last year. TrendForce data point to Q2 iPhone sales of 40 million, down from 61 million last year. iPhone SE sales are not helping much, trending at one third the rate of the ill-fated iPhone 5C. Estimates from TrendForce confirm the suspicions of many Apple (NASDAQ: AAPL ) watchers that iPhone sales are falling out of bed since the first of the year. TrendForce estimates Apple's market share plunged to 14.4% in the March quarter from 20.9% in the Christmas period. In the same interval, Samsung ( OTC:SSNLF ) seems to have added almost as much market share as Apple lost with gains also being made by Lenovo ( OTCPK:LNVGY ), LG ( OTC:LGEAF ) and Xiaomi (Private: XI ). Total shipments for the quarter show seasonal softness. The TrendForce estimates suggest Apple's Q2 volumes fell to the 40 million range, a substantial decline from last year's 61 million . The TrendForce estimates are bolstered by recent reports that Apple is cutting production of the iPhone 6S owing to swelling inventories and that the lower production rates may continue through next June. The recently released iPhone SE does not seem to be helping much. The lower-priced phone was apparently targeted at emerging markets like India. To date, however, Apple has shipped less than 25,000 iPhone SE models into India, according to data from zauba.com. India is not the only soft spot. Fiksu.com data show uptake of the iPhone SE flatlining at 0.3% of the iPhone installed base or about one-third as popular as the dismal reception of the iPhone 5C, Apple's last attempt to introduce a lower-priced phone. Apple fans will have to wait for the iPhone 7 to see if the slumping sales will be reversed. I would not expect too much. At least one analyst thinks Apple stock is headed into the $70s. The implied strategy of selling middling devices for premium prices seems to be catching up to Apple. Competitors such as Samsung, Huawei and Xiaomi have taken the gloves off by introducing truly superior devices at highly competitive prices, and consumers are getting the point. Why pay a huge premium for the Apple logo on an increasingly dated design with what is rapidly becoming a sub-par ecosystem? If AAPL's March-quarter release next week confirms the ugly picture, its stock will no doubt accelerate its southern vacation and the Apple fan club can plan on holding a wake rather than its typical quarterly earnings celebration.
