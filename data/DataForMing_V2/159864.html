Intel Silicon Photonics Is Back - Intel Corporation (NASDAQ:INTC) | Seeking Alpha Silicon Photonics enables data center connectivity (100 G to 400 G) TAM growth from $1.2 B in 2016 to $5.1 B in 2020. 51% CAGR in data center connectivity TAM. Intel Silicon Photonics extends high speed connectivity from 3 meters to 2 kilometers, facilitates higher density arrays, allows integration of communications into silicon and efficiently scales line rate speeds. Silicon Photonics to replace fiber optics and copper wires in data centers while making data centers more efficient in meeting end user needs through a flatter network hierarchy. For investors, Intel Silicon Photonics continues to grow Intel sales and margins in the Data Center and expands the Intel TAM further into data center communications. Intel (NASDAQ: INTC ) has brought back Silicon Photonics with an even larger TAM (Total Addressable Market) than introduced in 2014. Silicon Photonics was introduced by Intel in mid-2014 including presentations at IDF 14 and Intel Investor Day 2014. Then in early 2015 Intel announced that Silicon Photonics was being sent back to engineering to correct some issues found during testing. At the time Intel anticipated Silicon Photonics re-entering the market in late 2015. Along with many others, I was disappointed with the delays to Silicon Photonics but it is better to do it right than go forward with a product that has issues . . . especially in data centers where reliability and reputation are key (think six nines of reliability). Over the last nine months (with a new Silicon Photonics manager from outside Intel) Intel has improved upon their Silicon Photonics product and is now touting it as a cost effective replacement for fiber optics in much of the data center to increase communication speeds around and between data centers. In addition Intel is working on improvements to Silicon Photonics that will allow it to cost effectively replace more fiber optics as well as copper in the data center with faster communication speeds. With data centers Intel has increased the processing speed so much with Moore's Law that recently storage and connectivity have become constraints. Intel's introduction of 3D XPoint helps reduce the storage constraint and now Silicon Photonics helps to reduce the connectivity constraint. At the recent IDF15 I had a great conversation with a systems person from Facebook (NASDAQ: FB ). He described having hundreds of thousands of processor units in Facebook data centers. More data is sent across a single mega data center than across the entire World Wide Web. Obviously a three meter long high speed meter connection is a constraint. A two-kilometer Silicon Photonics cable with the same speed increases flexibility and throughput significantly. Silicon Photonics provides the data center with faster, denser and longer reach connectivity. Imagine the Facebook data center below in North Carolina filled with 120,000 Intel Xeon server units . . . not reaching their full potential due to constraints associated with connectivity. Silicon Photonics is a Key Enabler in the Transformation of Data Centers At one time fiber optics was thought to be the end all for communications. Now fiber optics is a constraint in the data center. Silicon Photonics takes what was previously performed in fiber optics modems and allows direct connections between silicon. Lithography is used to define lasers right on the silcon. This provides a huge advantage for Intel in future scaling of Silicon Photonics to smaller and smaller sizes producing higher and higher bandwidth and faster and faster speeds. The constraint associated with fiber optics is being replaced by Intel with the faster, more dense and longer reaching Silicon Photonics. The current connectivity constraints require a hierarchy of connections where a bank of processors (racks) communicates with two levels a set of supervisory processors (leaf and spine) which then communicate with a mother of all processors . . . the core. For one processor to communicate with another it must go through the leaf, through the spine to the core and then back through the spine, through the leaf and back to the rack. Each connection slows latency and uses power. Silicon Photonics allows one level of supervisory computers to handle all communications without first going through a leaf or checking in with the data center core. Eliminating these two layers improves network throughput significantly while descreasing power use. Intel will be connecting CPUs and switches directly with optics (Intel Silicon Photonics) to provide the greatest performance and best TCO (total cost of operation). Intel anticipates that sales in the Data Center Group over the next three years will increase at a 15% CAGR. During the same period the portion of these sales associated with communications is expected to double as a percentage of the total. The slide below shows a 51% CAGR in focus areas associated with communications in the data center. The potential for Intel Photonics looks to be approximately $1B in 2016 and another $4B in growth between 2016 and 2020. Intel will be commercially sampling their 100 Gb/s Silicon Photonics product this year and be in production in 2016 filling what is currently a $1 b need. Silicon Photonics will be a significant growth component for the Data Center Group over the next five years. Disclosure: I am/we are long INTC.
