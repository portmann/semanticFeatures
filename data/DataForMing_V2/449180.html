Boeing: Deferral Of Boeing 737MAX By Southwest Is Not A Bad Thing - The Boeing Company (NYSE:BA) | Seeking Alpha Deferral aligns the delivery schedule with growth schedules and requirement to replace aircraft. Southwest Airlines (NYSE: LUV ) has announced it will be delaying the delivery of the Boeing 737MAX to preserve cash. While the airline has delayed as much as 40% of their outstanding Boeing 737 MAX8 order, it still will be the launch customer for the aircraft. In this article I will have a look at the delay and explain why I do not expect this to be a structural problem for Boeing (NYSE: BA ). Before having a look at the deferral of deliveries, it is important to understand why an airline would even think about deferring delays. The different forecasts, provided by Boeing and Airbus, have one thing in common: In the coming 20 years the air travel market will boom once again. So the question you might ask yourself is "if the market is booming, why would you even think about deferring?". The answer to that is actually pretty easy, but it has does have some facets. One of the reasons to defer deliveries could be overcapacity. Ordering aircraft happens with a certain growth pattern in mind. Overcapacity can occur when short term growth rates fall short of expectations, while airlines maintain the predefined delivery schedule. Figures from 2015 and the start of 2016 show that on average legacy carriers have increased their capacity more than they were able to realize growth in the number of carried passengers. The capacity growth outpaced the traffic growth by roughly 0%-2%. Capacity growth for Southwest has been in line with traffic growth in Q1 2016. There are a lot of variables that can change by the time you have to take delivery of the airframe and I think that is what happened in the case of Southwest. Figure 1: Orders placed for the Boeing 737MAX by Southwest versus oil prices (Source: www.AeroAnalysis.net ) In December 2011, Southwest ordered 150 aircraft and became the launch customer for the Boeing 737MAX. The company added orders and now has 30 MAX7 and 170 MAX8 aircraft on order. The company secured early spots, which is something you would do when there is an urge for having the airframes available as soon as possible. Capacity expansion or cost reduction could be some reasons to try to secure early spots. In the case of Southwest (and other carriers), the airframes were ordered when oil prices were high. However, since the purchase of the airframes the fuel prices have declined by roughly 50%. An airline does not only pay fuel costs, but also has capital costs so there is a tradeoff that needs to be made here between capital costs and fuel costs. Currently it is likely that the advantage of lower fuel costs does not outweigh the higher capital costs related to newer and more efficient aircraft. AeroAnalysis estimated insurance costs to be $0.1 million higher per airframe per year for the Boeing 737MAX on top of being already $5.6 million more expensive compared to buying a Boeing 737-800. The saving in fuel costs are estimated to be $0.8 million per year. What becomes clear is that savings in fuel costs are not high enough to justify taking delivery of the Boeing 737MAX now. The Boeing 737-800s in the Southwest fleet are young, so the MAX will be used as a -700 replacement. Comparing the Boeing 737-700 with the Boeing 737 MAX8, the insurance costs for the MAX 8 would be $0.2 million higher annually, while the aircraft is already $11.8 million more expensive. Additionally, the fuel costs are higher as well. What becomes clear is that with the current fuel prices, the Boeing 737MAX is not a very appealing airplane. Especially for airlines that have a big number of Boeing 737MAX aircraft on order and do have sufficient airframes in their fleet or cheaper alternatives on order, deferral is a step that should not surprise anybody. Figure 2: Delivery schedule Southwest Airlines (Source: www.investors.southwest.com ) Southwest aims to grow its fleet by roughly 2% annually through 2018. At the end of 2015, the carrier has 704 aircraft in service. A two percent increase implies a net addition of 14-15 aircraft annually through 2018. By 2018 the airline will have roughly 750 aircraft in service. In the outer right column, it can be seen that through 2018 169 (including 18 options) deliveries were scheduled. Sticking to the 2% growth scheme, this means that Southwest would have to phase out 123 aircraft through 2018. In the first phase, 2016-2017/2018, of the fleet revision the Classic 737s will be removed from service. In the second phase that starts in 2023 the older Boeing 737-700 will be removed from service. The airline has now chosen to defer deliveries of 67 737MAX aircraft that were scheduled for delivery between 2019-2022 and accelerate the delivery of the current generation aircraft. The earlier retirement of the 737 Classic aircraft should increase revenue and cut costs for the relatively old airframes. In total Southwest had 162 scheduled MAX deliveries for the 2019-2022 timeframe. This means that over 40% of the aircraft that were scheduled for delivery have been delayed. One thing that Boeing investors might ask themselves is "Should I worry?". The answer is simple: "No". The deferral by Southwest is related to a few things. Low fuel prices make the Boeing 737MAX an expensive the replacement compared to current generation aircraft. Additionally, Southwest has a certain fleet and capacity growth target in mind, replacing the smaller Boeing 737-700 with the bigger MAX8 would certainly result in unwanted capacity growth. A last reason for Southwest to defer deliveries until after 2023 is the economic viable life of the first Boeing 737-700 being reached in that year. In short: the deferral is a result of Southwest aligning their delivery schedule with the actual need to replace aging aircraft. What can be said is that the decision to defer is a healthy one by Southwest to squeeze the maximum out of their current 737-700 aircraft, avoid higher capital costs and maintain healthy capital expenditures. For Boeing this does not say anything about demand for the aircraft. For some airlines the lower fuel prices directly have led to a lower urge to introduce fuel efficient aircraft. But there are enough airlines that actually need the MAX aircraft to support their fleet expansion. So for Boeing the deferral is not a bad thing and the company will not have any problems filling the slots that became available.
