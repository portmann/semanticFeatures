Why Softbank's $90 Billion In Debt Isn't That Big Of A Deal - SOFTBANK Corp.ADR (OTCMKTS:SFTBY) | Seeking Alpha To screen ETFs by asset class, performance, yield and more, check out the ETF Hub.ETF Hub Softbank bears point to its debt position as a deal breaker for buying its stock.While high, Softbank's debt is actually among the lowest of any major telecom company when accounting for cash.It occurred to me after recently writing my very bullish Softbank piece , which looks at both its operations and investments, that bears don't necessarily argue against Softbank's (OTCPK: SFTBY ) lucrative investments or fast growth, but rather its debt.Softbank has $90 billion in debt, and to many investors, that debt is a deal breaker.However, it's not that big of a deal.SFTBY Total Long Term Debt (Quarterly) data by YCharts The chart above may look a bit confusing, but what it shows is Softbank, Verizon (NYSE: VZ ), and AT&T's (NYSE: T ) debt and cash position.Clearly, Verizon has the most debt on its balance sheet, but if we subtract its cash position then it has $102.6 billion in debt.When accounting for cash, AT&T has the second most debt at $73.4 billion followed by Softbank at $68.8 billion.This is the first illustration that Softbank's debt position isn't that big of a deal.In retrospect, its debt position relative to cash is more attractive than most large telecom companies throughout the globe, such as China Mobile (NYSE: CHL ), who has a debt position minus cash of well over $72 billion.VZ Net Income (NYSE: TTM) data by YCharts The second chart, above, looks at a company's ability to pay off that debt.Net income is a company's bottom line.Sure, it is often a reflection of various one time expenses, but with big dividend payments, sometimes one time events can hinder a company's ability to make debt payments and pay out big dividends, which is why I think seeing a company's income minus dividends is a good metric of determining the chances that debt eventually gets paid.As seen, Verizon barely has a profit after dividend payments, and AT&T's dividends are 50% higher than its income.Meanwhile, Softbank has $7 billion left to do whatever it wishes.Another big difference is that Softbank operates in a Japanese telecom market that allowed it to grow revenue by more than 40% during the last nine months of last year.Verizon and AT&T are struggling for mid-single digit growth thanks to pricing wars within a mature U.S. telecom market, which has also strangled margins.That said, Softbank does have the 1,000 pound ankle weight that is Sprint (NYSE: S ), and it should be noted that much of Softbank's debt is associated with Softbank's 80% ownership of Sprint.However, like the U.S., Japan has a low interest rate environment, which is why Softbank's last two rounds of debt (over $3 billion each) have a 2.5% coupon over seven years.For this reason, I see no scenario where Softbank's debt becomes a long-term problem, because even if the Sprint investment were to dwindle down to a zero dollar value, Softbank is still fully capable of making debt payments and growing larger as its investments materialize and its business grows.Editor's Note: This article discusses one or more securities that do not trade on a major U.S. exchange.Please be aware of the risks associated with these stocks.Source: Why Softbank's $90
