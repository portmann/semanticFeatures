Big Banks Are Not Nationalized If They Can't Crash | Seeking Alpha Critics continue to claim the Fed "stress tests" called CCAR are a burden.They are, but banks that pass deserve your money, and those that don't, don't.People who like to see banks take the depositors' money to the dog track, like analyst Richard Bove , now claim that the nation's biggest banks have been "nationalized" by having to pass a Comprehensive Capital Analysis and Review, or CCAR "stress test" before handing out more money to shareholders.Bove, you may recall, is the genius who stuck a "buy" rating on Lehman Brothers as late as August, 2008 .Listening to such voices about bank regulation at this point is like listening to a convicted spree killer on gun safety.The fact is that banks insure deposits up to $100,000, through the Federal Government.In order for that insurance to matter, the government needs to be assured it's unlikely to need to cover that insurance, something that wasn't the case before noted liberal George W. Bush signed the Economic Stabilization Act , which set the process leading to the "stress tests" in motion.Most banks are having no problem with this year's tests, which evaluate how bank capital can handle economic volatility.Capital One (NYSE: COF ) raised its dividend 33% after passing the test.Citicorp (NYSE: C ) passed the test and announced a dividend.Regions Financial (NYSE: RF ) raised its dividend 20% after passing the test.Not all banks have done so well.Bank of America (NYSE: BAC ) was told it has to re-submit before it can raise its dividends.Deutsche Bank (NYSE: DB ) and Bank Santander (NYSE: SAN ) failed.Deposit insurance is a form of government protection, and for that protection government has a right to look at the books, just as any private party offering similar protection would have a right to look at the books.When those examinations were incomplete, as they were in the last decade, huge banks like Bank of America required government bailouts to survive.Without such bailouts we would almost certainly have revisited the Great Depression's horrors.Learning from history is not a bad thing.It's true that, since the stress tests first came into force in 2010, regional banks have for the most part been outperforming money center banks.Wells Fargo (NYSE: WFC ), a money center bank whose policies are more like those of a regional, has been the star of the banking show, its stock nearly doubling in that time.Companies that combine pool risky trading ventures with deposit activity, on a massive scale, like Bank of America and Citicorp, have not done so well.One would think that the lesson by now is clear.Don't take the depositors' money to the dog track.Don't put the government's guarantee at risk.Goldman Sachs (NYSE: GS ) CEO Lloyd Blankfein acknowledges that there's no longer such a thing as "pure capitalism," and that western banking systems are regulated.He is right.After the 1913 creation of the Federal Reserve, the Great Depression, and the Great Recession, the message of regulation has finally sunk in, but its necessity has not.Here is my bottom line.The CCAR process is working, and I wouldn't touch a bank stock that
