Favorable USD Data Prelude March 2015 FOMC Meeting | Seeking Alpha ISM Manufacturing and Core PCE disappointed, but they are not expected to last.Dallas Fed has an alternative inflation reading that is closer to the 2% inflation target.Strong labor market data is seen as sustainable and led Dallas Fed President Fisher to make a bullish claim on U.S. economy.After ranging in February 2015, the USD is now on its ascendancy with all eyes on upcoming FOMC statement on 18 March 2015.The United States Dollar (USD) remains as the premier currency of our time despite numerous predictions of other currencies like the euro or Japanese yen taking over the role of the USD as the reserve currency.Hence it is a natural starting point for us to look at the USD in our currency analysis.There are several hits and miss this month when it comes to the USD.Let us start with the miss.A notable miss is that the ISM Manufacturing PMI came in lesser than expected.This survey of leading manufacturers in the United States showed that they are still overall optimistic, they are feeling the pain of the prolonged West Coast strike and low energy prices.This resulted in a 0.6 point drop of the ISM to 52.9 for March 2015, and we can see from the chart above that ISM manufacturing is on the downtrend since 5 months ago.However it is noted that both the strike and low energy prices are transitory, so it is likely that the Fed will pass it in their monetary policy decision-making process.Another notable miss is the crucial inflation reading.The Fed has defined the inflation target of 2% as the standard for price stability in pursuit of its mandate.There are many inflation measures in the world right now, the most widely used inflation reading is the Consumer Price Index (CPI) which measures the change in prices in a basket of consumer goods over time.This is favored by the Bank of England and the official measure of inflation.The Fed has adopted the Personal Consumption Expenditure (PCE) which measures a different basket of consumption than the CPI, with different methodology.Core PCE removes the more volatile element of food and energy prices, is now seen as leading indicator, and it is expected that headline PCE will converge to Core PCE as the transient low energy prices pass.This is the second bad news this month and we see that core PCE is only at 0.1%.So it is apparent that the Fed has a dilemma.The labor market is nearing full employment, a point which we will cover in detail later with the latest employment report, but the inflation measures remain far from its 2% target.Fear not.Creativity is the mother of necessity.The Dallas Fed deserves the most creative award and they came up with the notion of trimmed mean PCE which is an alternative measure to the core PCE.Trimmed mean PCE also excludes the volatile food and energy prices with data from the Bureau of Economic Analysis.The Dallas Fed came up with the following results.While the monthly trimmed mean PCE is negative, the 12 months PCE inflation is now at a respectable 1.5% for January 2015.This means that the Fed expects to see inflation of 1.5% when oil prices return back its normal price above $100/barrel before the supply glut pushed it down to a low of $45/barrel in January 2015.Lastly, we move on to the hit of the U.S. economy and it is a major hit.The Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) showed that the economy added 295,000 new jobs in February 2015 and pushed down unemployment from 5.7% to 5.5% past expectations of 5.6%.This surprisingly strong report pushed in into the natural employment range of 5.2% to 5.5% as defined in the recent January 2015 FOMC minutes.This recovery has even lead noted hawk, Dallas Fed President Richard Fisher to see the U.S. as the fast horse in the global race track.You can read more about it here .Hence for this month of March, the notable misses are seen as transitory and hit in the labor market is seen as sustainable as the economic recovery continues.The U.S. is expected to grow between 2%-3% this year.As to whether this optimism is warranted, only time will tell.In the meantime, the USD has continued to strengthen on the assumption that the U.S. growth momentum will continue.Economic and Monetary Divergence - Key Strengths of USD It is this divergence in growth momentum that is powering the strength of the USD.China has revised its growth target to 7% in 2015 after missing its 7.5% growth target in 2014 by 0.1%.Other major economies like Australia and Canada are threatened by the low energy prices, as they are energy producers and they too expect lower growth this year.Europe is still struck in an uncertain future of economic growth and while there might be signs of sporadic recovery, we cannot be sure that this recovery will be sustained at such an early stage and the same can be said for Japan.Beyond the different pace of economic growth, we are continuing to see divergence in monetary policy.For instance, the European Central Bank (ECB) has started to implement its $60 billion euro/ month of monetary easing policy this month and its total easing package is expected to be $1.14 trillion euros.The Bank of Japan (BOJ) continues on its $80 trillion yen of monetary easing and it is buying 90% of the Japanese Government Bonds being issued.This is in addition to the easing by the central banks of China, India, Australia and Canada in the past 3 months.Most importantly, the favorable data this month has lead to market expectations to consolidate to mid 2015 liftoff consensus or even earlier.This is after some expectations of September 2015 liftoff by some notable investors.The market will be on the lookout for the 17-18 March 2015 FOMC meeting and the statement at the end of the meeting on 18 March 2015.We can have a measure of the USD strength through the PowerShares DB USD Bull ETF (NYSEARCA: UUP ).The UUP tracks the performance of the USD against Euro, Japanese Yen, British Pound, Canadian Dollar, Swedish Krona and Swiss Franc through the Deutsche Bank Long US Dollar Index.The UUP chart below shows that the USD has resumed its uptrend after it stalled in February 2015.It is a universally known fact that nothing goes up forever.The USD has been on a bull run for 7 months from July 2014 to January 2015.After 7 months of strong run, it is remarkable that the break in its uptrend resulted in the range of the USD and not in decline which I had expected.The strong labor market report spiked the gap up 2 candles ago, as seen in the daily chart above.Considering the notable hits and misses of the USD this month, I would expect to see a gradual strengthening of the USD for at least this month and another 2 months ahead.Source: Favorable USD Data Prelude March 2015
